01 📅 Weekly Rhythm Every Week

Two meetings every week — different format, different purpose, neither optional. The offline session is for deep work and presence. The online session is for accessibility and accountability. Working students can always attend at least one.

🏢 Friday Offline Session
On-campus every Friday.
  • instructor-led technical session (Go mastery, algorithms, tools) and student project presentations + peer Q&A (see Section 4)
  • Each session is about 90 minutes — no extensions, no exceptions
  • Attendance is tracked — missing 3 consecutive Fridays = warning
🗓 Every Friday  ·  On-campus  ·  2:00 PM → 8:00 PM
💻 Online Weekly Meetup
Midweek online session — open to all students regardless of schedule. Q&A, checkpoint discussions, and buddy check-ins.
  • Open Q&A: any topic from the current exam month
  • Checkpoint status: buddy pairs report progress to the group
  • One student shares something they learned that week — 5 minutes
  • Recorded and posted for students who couldn't attend
🗓 Every Wednesday  ·  Online  ·  8:00 PM → 11:00 PM

02 🎯 Monthly Checkpoints 2 Per Month

Two checkpoints every month — not exams, not grades. A structured moment to pause, reflect, and know exactly where you stand. Your buddy reviews yours before it's submitted. You review theirs. The end-of-month session closes the loop and opens the next chapter.

Week 1–2
Checkpoint 1 — Mid-month pulse
Covers the first two weeks of the exam curriculum. Your buddy reviews your checkpoint answers before you submit — they flag anything unclear or incorrect. Opens Friday, closes Sunday online meetup.
Week 3–4
Checkpoint 2 — Month-end mastery check
Covers the full month's curriculum. Harder than Checkpoint 1. Same buddy review process — buddy must leave at least 2 written comments before you submit.
Last Friday
End-of-Month Session
Offline session with a different format. Instructor reveals what the next month's curriculum covers and why it matters. Students share one thing they understood differently by the end of the month. Competition winner announced. 30 minutes only.
🤝 The Buddy System
Each student is paired with one other for the entire month. You review each other's checkpoints. You are each other's first line of accountability.
  • Pairs assigned by instructor at the start of each month — rotates every month
  • Buddy must review and leave written comments before you submit
  • If your buddy is stuck, you are expected to help — not give answers
  • Pairs present together at the online meetup checkpoint discussion
🔄 New pairs every month  ·  Rotates so you meet everyone
📋 What Checkpoints Test
Not just code. Checkpoints test whether you can explain what you built — why you chose an approach, what the complexity is, what you would do differently.
  • Implement the required function correctly
  • Write a comment explaining your approach in plain language
  • State the time and space complexity of your solution
  • Name one alternative approach and why you didn't use it
📌 Submitted via 01Edu platform  ·  Opens Sunday, closes Friday
03 🔍 The Audit System Reformed

Most peer audits fail because the auditor doesn't know what to look for. These rules fix that. An audit is not a check — it's a conversation. The auditor learns as much as the person being audited. The final audit of every project is always offline — face to face.

Rules for Giving an Effective Audit
These rules apply to every audit — checkpoint, project, or exam.
  • Read before you judge. Run the code. Understand what it does before you comment on how it does it.
  • Ask, don't tell. "Why did you use a map here instead of a slice?" teaches more than "you should use a slice."
  • Comment on the thinking, not the person. "This approach is O(n²)" not "you wrote slow code."
  • Find one thing that is good. Every solution has something worth noting. Name it first.
  • Leave the audit better than you found it. Your comments must be actionable — not just observations.
The Audit Checklist
Use this for every peer audit. You must address all 5 points.
  • Correctness: Does the solution pass all test cases including edge cases?
  • Complexity: Is the time and space complexity what was required?
  • Readability: Can you understand the code without asking the author?
  • Comments: Does the author explain their reasoning, not just what the code does?
  • Rules: Were any forbidden functions or packages used?
⏱ Minimum 20 minutes per audit  ·  Written comments required

04 🎤 Friday Project Presentations weekly

Every other Friday, teams present their current project to the entire cohort. The instructor selects teams — you do not volunteer. This simulates a real engineering environment where you present when you're called on, not when you feel ready.

📌 Format
  • 10 minutes to present, 10 minutes Q&A — no exceptions
  • Demo first — show the working thing before explaining how it works
  • No slides required — whiteboard and terminal are enough
  • Both team members must speak — not just the strongest one
🗓 Even Fridays  ·  2–3 teams per session
❓ What the Audience Must Do
  • Every student must ask at least one question per session
  • Questions must be technical — not compliments
  • Challenge the approach: "why not do X instead of Y?"
  • The best question of the session is noted by the instructor
👥 Whole cohort attends  ·  Active participation required
📊 What Gets Evaluated
  • Can you explain your solution to someone who didn't write it?
  • Can you defend a decision under pressure?
  • Do you know what you would do differently next time?
  • Can your teammate explain your part as well as you can?
💬 Instructor gives written feedback same day
05 🏆 Monthly Competition Every Month

One competition every month. A theme is announced at the start of the month. Teams of 2–3 build and submit by the last Friday. The winner is rewarded and featured publicly — on the school's social media and the GitHub organisation.

🗓 Timeline
  • Day 1: Theme announced at start-of-month session
  • Week 1–3: Teams build alongside regular curriculum
  • Week 4, Wednesday: Submissions close on 01Edu
  • Last Friday: Teams present (5 min each), panel votes, winner announced
🗓 Announced Month Day 1  ·  Judged Last Friday
🏅 Judging Criteria
  • Working demo (40%): Does it run? Does it do what it claims?
  • Code quality (30%): Readable, well-structured, no obvious hacks
  • Defence (20%): Can the team explain every decision under Q&A?
  • Originality (10%): Did they take the theme somewhere unexpected?
👨‍⚖️ Panel: instructor + 2 senior students from previous cohort
🎁 Rewards
  • Project featured on school's LinkedIn and GitHub organisation page
  • Winner's name and photo posted publicly — builds their online presence
  • Project added permanently to 01edu-cairo GitHub org
  • Physical reward determined by school management each month
📢 Posted publicly within 24 hours of the last Friday session
📋 Rules
  • Teams of 2–3 — solo entries not accepted
  • Must use Go — the language of the curriculum
  • No copying from tutorials — code must be written during the month
  • All team members must be able to explain all parts of the project
⚠️ Projects failing the defence round are disqualified
06 🌍 Community Infrastructure Permanent

Three permanent systems that outlast any individual student. Each one builds a record of what this cohort accomplished — visible to employers, future students, and the Egyptian tech community.

🐙 School GitHub Org
A public GitHub organisation — 01edu-cairo — that acts as the school's permanent portfolio.
  • Every competition winner's project is forked in
  • Every Friday presentation project is linked
  • Students can point employers directly to the org
  • Contribution graph of the whole cohort — public proof of work
🔗 github.com/01edu-cairo  ·  Public forever
📝 State of the Cohort
One LinkedIn post per month. What the cohort learned, who won the competition, what's coming next. Written by a rotating student — not always the instructor.
  • Published on the last day of each month
  • Written by a different student each month — chosen by the instructor
  • Covers: curriculum topic, competition winner, one standout moment
  • Builds the writer's LinkedIn presence and the school's reputation
📅 Last day of each month  ·  LinkedIn + school channels
🤝 Buddy System
Each student is paired with one other for the entire month. You review each other's checkpoints, attend the online session together, and are each other's first point of contact when stuck.
  • Pairs rotate every month — you will pair with everyone over 6 months
  • Buddy review is required before checkpoint submission
  • If either buddy misses the online session, they owe the other a written summary
  • Best buddy pair of the month recognised at end-of-month session
🔄 New pairs assigned every Month Day 1
07 🎙️ Future Workshops Coming Soon

Once the weekly rhythm and accountability systems are established and running smoothly — months 3 or 4 onward — we bring in the outside world. The community needs to be strong enough to make the most of external guests.

🧑‍💻 Software Engineer Sessions
Real engineers from Egyptian tech companies — doing the exact job students want. Format: 20-minute talk, 20-minute student Q&A.
  • Target companies: Instabug, Paymob, Breadfast, Swvl, Fawry, Vodafone Digital
  • Students prepare 2 real questions in advance — required
  • Speaker connected to students on LinkedIn after every session
  • Scheduled when community rhythm is established (Month 3+)
🗓 Monthly from Month 3  ·  Wednesday evening
🧠 Soft Skills Workshops
Professional instructors covering the human side of engineering — communication, CV writing, interview skills, negotiation.
  • CV workshop: what Egyptian tech recruiters actually look for
  • Technical communication in English — writing and speaking
  • Interview simulation with professional feedback
  • All sessions Saturday afternoon — accessible for working students
🗓 Monthly from Month 4  ·  Saturday afternoon
